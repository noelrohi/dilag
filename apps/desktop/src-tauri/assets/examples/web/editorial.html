Every letter my grandfather never sent

A shoebox of thirty-one envelopes, each addressed but unstamped, sat on the top shelf of a closet in Scranton for forty-two years. What they contained was, in a way, the shape of an entire life.

Eleanor Vance
April 12, 2026

The box was blue, the kind of department-store blue that doesn't exist anymore, and it had been labeled in my grandmother's small, exacting handwriting: George — personal. She had left instructions that it not be opened until after both of them were gone. My grandfather outlived her by six years, and when he died in the winter of 2024, I was the one who found it.

Inside were thirty-one letters, each in its own envelope, each addressed in my grandfather's hand to a woman named Ada. None of them had ever been sent. The earliest was dated 1962. The latest, 1997.

I read them in the order they were written. This took three days. I will not reproduce any of them here, not because the content is especially private — it is tender and ordinary, as most private things are — but because what struck me was not what they said individually, but what they said together.

"There is a kind of devotion that asks nothing in return, and another kind that asks only that it never be seen. I am not sure which is the better kind."

What the letters said, collectively, was this: that for most of his adult life, my grandfather had been quietly in love with someone who was not his wife. He had never acted on it. Ada had, I later learned, married a man from New Brunswick in 1963 and died in 2009, unaware. The letters were not a record of an affair. They were a record of a restraint.
